The Vanderbilt Quantum Initiative (VQI) represents a multidisciplinary approach to solving some of the world's most complex computational challenges. Within VQI, the Finance Team focuses strictly on where quantum algorithms intersect with capital markets—seeking distinct computational advantages in areas such as portfolio optimization, risk analysis, and derivative pricing.

By translating theoretical physics into pragmatic financial models, we aim to bridge the gap between tomorrow's quantum hardware capabilities and today's institutional finance requirements.

The Challenge of Option Pricing

Classical option pricing primarily relies on the Monte Carlo simulation. While extremely versatile for modeling complex derivatives and stochastic processes (like the Black-Scholes-Merton model), Monte Carlo methods converge slowly. Specifically, they suffer from a convergence rate of O(1/√N), meaning that to reduce the pricing error by a factor of 10, you must run 100 times as many simulations.

For institutions pricing large books of complex exotic options in real-time, this computational bottleneck requires massive, energy-intensive classical computing clusters that still take hours to run overnight risk calculations.

Quantum Amplitude Estimation (QAE)

Quantum Amplitude Estimation is a fundamental quantum algorithm that provides a solution to the Monte Carlo bottleneck. Instead of classically averaging random simulation paths, QAE takes an abstract classical function, loads it into a quantum state space, and estimates its expected amplitude value through quantum phase estimation and Grover's search operator.

The Quadratic Speedup

QAE achieves a convergence rate of roughly O(1/N). This indicates a quadratic speedup over classical Monte Carlo. A risk calculation requiring 1,000,000 classical simulations to reach a specific accuracy would theoretically require only 1,000 quantum operations to achieve the exact same precision level.

How We Apply It

Our team utilizes IBM's QisKit framework to physically build the circuit architectures necessary for QAE in option pricing:

  • Distribution Loading: We map the mathematical probability distribution of the underlying asset (such as the Log-Normal distribution for typical equities) directly into the superposition of the working qubits.
  • Payoff Function Circuit: We construct specialized quantum comparators and controlled-Y rotations to calculate the option's specific payoff at maturity.
  • Amplitude Estimation: We execute the QAE algorithm to measure the probability amplitude of the resulting target state, effectively extracting the expected theoretical price of the option without simulating infinite paths.
